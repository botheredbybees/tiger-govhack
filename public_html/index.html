Keeping our waterways healthy

Stormwater from the Glenorchy City Council areas flow into the Derwent mostly via the stormwater pipes. (see map – need to get Alex Leith to reattach the link to the GCC stormwater pipes data). Pollution such as litter, chemicals, fertiliser and pet faeces is often transported in stormwater into the Estuary. Managing stormwater quality is critical to managing the health of the estuary, the focus of the Derwent Estuary Program.

A healthy Derwent Estuary is foundational to the quality of life of not only the Glenorchy City Council community but of a much wider group of Australians. The connection in providing a pleasing and healthy environment for local people in which to live is obvious. However the estuary is also habitat to many species that migrate across Australia and even further afield, such as Australian Salmon and Short-tailed Shearwaters. Consider that the estuary is a nursery for Australian Salmon, that are then a favoured Angling species as they migrate along the Eastern and western Australian coastlines.

There are a number of ways open government data provided in an online interactive mapping platform could assist the community to work together to manage stormwater quality. Firstly providing a map of the stormwater network in relation to the Derwent shows clearly the nature, size and scale of the situation. Local GCC community members can locate their shere of activity in relation to this: where, they live, work shop and play. In this way their role in the issue becomes more transparent. Community members then become informed actors, able to change how their behavour contributes to the problem or solution. For example, people may chose not to do actions that pollute the stormwater: litter, allow their pets to defecate anywhere, wantonly spray garden chemicals and spread fertiliser, or dump chemicals. They may also act constructively such as by monitoring water quality, collecting rubbish and other pollutants, or working together to install pollution control devices

Secondly they can contribute to the information ecosystem by adding related information. This could be reporting pollution incidents, publishing monitoring data, sharing “good news” stories such as working bees. This information adds to the overall story, further informing the community and providing a basis for informed action. It can also contribute to the Local and State Governments knowledge base, providing valuable information they can then integrate with their datasets and re-publish. It also provides them with further information on what is happening and how and where their resources should be best deployed.
